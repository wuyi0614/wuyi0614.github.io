The news article points readers to our publication in Nature Energy, "The heterogeneous role of energy policies in the energy transition of Asia-Pacific emerging economies," by emphasizing the regional heterogeneity of energy transitions in the Asia-Pacific emerging economies. In this article, it highlights that legal mandates and climate-policy commitments matter, but their impacts are shaped by starting conditions: existing energy systems, institutional capacity, technology endowments, and economic structures can all affect how quickly low-carbon transitions unfold.

Our finding is important because it frames energy policy not as a single universal lever, but as a context-dependent mechanism whose effectiveness depends on regional development pathways, industrial structure, and policy design. For sustainable finance and climate-policy research, this perspective is especially useful: capital allocation, transition risk, and public policy must be evaluated with attention to local constraints rather than assuming that the same regulation will deliver the same decarbonization response everywhere. 

This news also situates the Asia-Pacific study within broader debates on sustainable development goals, climate targets, and energy-system transformation. It also reinforces the need for data-rich, policy-sensitive analysis when assessing climate finance instruments and energy-transition pathways.
